Balkrishna - The Divine Child

The Birth of Krishna

Krishna was born to Devaki and Vasudeva in Mathura, where his parents were imprisoned by the tyrant king, Kamsa. The birth of Krishna was surrounded by miraculous events, allowing Vasudeva to safely transfer the infant Krishna to Gokul, where he was raised by Nanda and Yashoda.

Life in Gokul and Vrindavan

Balkrishna’s early life in Gokul and Vrindavan is filled with stories of divine playfulness, miracles, and the deep affection shared by the villagers for the young deity.

Makhan Chor (Butter Thief)

Krishna’s love for butter led him to steal it from the homes of the Gopis, earning him the nickname “Makhan Chor”. Despite his mischief, the Gopis adored him, leaving butter out for Krishna to steal.

Kaliya Mardan (Subduing the Serpent Kaliya)

When the serpent Kaliya poisoned the Yamuna River, Krishna, as a young child, fearlessly jumped into the river, danced on the serpent’s hoods, and forced it to leave, saving the villagers.

Protecting the Villagers

Krishna protected the villagers of Gokul and Vrindavan from many threats, including lifting the massive Govardhan Hill on his little finger to shield them from a storm sent by Indra.

The Love of Yashoda

Krishna’s foster mother, Yashoda, shared a deep, unconditional love for him. Her attempts to discipline the mischievous child often resulted in tender moments that highlighted the divine nature of their relationship.

Bala Leelas (Childhood Plays)

Krishna’s childhood was full of “Leelas”, divine plays that carried deep spiritual significance. His interactions with his friends, the Gopas and Gopis, and his love for animals, especially cows, depicted his divine nature.

Balkrishna in Hindu Philosophy and Devotion

Balkrishna’s stories hold deep philosophical and spiritual meaning, symbolizing various aspects of divine play, innocence, devotion, and universal love.

“Balkrishna embodies the concept of Lila, the divine play that underlies the universe.”

Symbol of Divine Playfulness

Balkrishna’s stories emphasize “Lila”, the divine play that represents the joy and wonder of life, encouraging devotees to approach life with a sense of playfulness and joy.

Embodiment of Innocence and Purity

Krishna’s childlike innocence and purity are seen as reflections of the pure, untainted nature of the soul, free from ego and worldly complexities.

Ideal of Bhakti (Devotion)

The Gopis’ unconditional love for Krishna represents the highest form of Bhakti, where the devotee’s love for God is all-consuming and personal.

Representation of Universal Love

Balkrishna’s relationships with all beings, regardless of their nature, remind us of the all-encompassing nature of divine love.

Balkrishna in Art and Culture

Balkrishna has inspired countless forms of artistic and cultural expression, from iconography and festivals to music, dance, and literature.

Iconography

Balkrishna is a popular subject in Indian art, often depicted holding a pot of butter or playing the flute, symbolizing his playful and divine nature.

Festivals and Celebrations

Krishna’s birthday, Janmashtami, is celebrated with great fervor, including the popular “Dahi Handi” tradition that reenacts his butter-stealing exploits.

Music and Dance

Classical music and dance forms, such as Bharatanatyam and Kathak, often feature performances based on Krishna’s childhood, celebrating the divine child through artistic expression.

Literature

The stories of Balkrishna have been passed down through generations in oral traditions, poetry, and literature, inspiring countless works across different cultures and eras.

Balkrishna and the Bhakti Movement

The Bhakti movement, which emphasized personal devotion to God, played a significant role in popularizing the worship of Balkrishna.

Vallabhacharya and Pushtimarg

Vallabhacharya established the Pushtimarg sect, focusing on the worship of Balkrishna, where the relationship between the devotee and God is seen as one of nurturing love.

Mirabai and Her Devotion

Mirabai, a famous female saint of the Bhakti movement, expressed her deep devotion to Krishna through songs and poems, celebrating his child form with affection.
